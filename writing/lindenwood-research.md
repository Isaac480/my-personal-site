During the summer after my freshman year at UChicago, I had the opportunity to conduct **mathematics research** under Lindenwood University. This research had two main components; the first entailed conducting a **literature review on the corequisite support** model of teaching; the second analyzing real-world student **course outcomes** of mathematics classes.
## Corequisite Support
Why do so many college undergraduates struggle to succeed in college level math? Researchers increasingly have pinned the blame on prerequisite education, which places underprepared students into **developmental courses** before they can enroll in credit-bearing math. Recent work in math education has identified a number of **shortcomings** of the developmental model, including feelings of pointlessness, emphasis on menial procedural skills, and a stigma of inferiority. Besides all of these theoretical implications, research has shown students taking developmental courses do not show substantial improvements in grades compared to their peers who are directly placed into college-level courses. 

One promising alternative that has emerged is **corequisite education**, a model where students *simultaneously take a college-level, credit-bearing math course, while also receiving targeted academic support through a support course*. For my internship, I was tasked with **compiling the literature** on the corequisite support model, which yielded a number of findings:

* increases the number of students who complete college math classes
* decreases withdrawal rates, and leads to an overall increase in college credit
* shows potential for increasing degree completion rates and enhancing long-term career success
* enables students to enter college as full members of the community
* positive instructor relationships in corequisite structures reduced anxiety and increased confidence

While these findings are promising, corequisite support may not work for students who are significantly underprepared and who may need remedial coursework.

## Analyzing Course Outcomes

The other major component of this internship was a **quantitative analysis** of student course outcomes in three courses – *Statistics, Intermediate Algebra, and College Algebra*. By running chi-squared tests, I was able to compare student grades with a number of factors, including:

- instruction mode (online/in person)
- risk score (based on personal factors)
- grade level
- ethnicity
- gender
- pell status
- tutoring received

This analysis allowed me to generate an **actionable plan** to increase student learning outcomes. For example, we identified members of certain ethnicities and genders as being particularly prone to struggling, which means that a concerted effort to support these groups would be the most cost-effective. Additionally, we were able to show that students who received tutoring were more likely to earn A/B grades than those who didn't, and that the frequency of tutoring was positively correlated with grades.
